25 Jun 2026 Long Gilts

Oil, Inflation, and a UK Rates Trade — What the US–Iran MOU Means for Gilts

The News On June 15, the US and Iran signed an MOU ending the conflict that had shut the Strait of Hormuz — a chokepoint carrying roughly 20% of global oil supply. Oil fell to three-month lows on the news. The deal is not final — Iran's nuclear programme, sanctions, and Hormuz logistics remain unresolved within a 60-day window.

Why This Hits the UK Hardest Energy carries a larger weight in the UK inflation basket than in the eurozone — UK households spend proportionally more on gas and electricity, so oil moves show up more sharply in UK data. Falling headline inflation gives the BoE justification to cut rates faster than currently expected, and the data now supports a move the market hasn't fully priced in. When rate cut expectations are pulled forward, investors buy gilts in anticipation — prices rise, yields fall.

The Trade Long UK 10-year gilts, short German 10-year bunds. The ECB has already been cutting more aggressively than the BoE this year, meaning most of the dovish repricing in European bonds has already happened — bunds have less room to rally from here. Germany is also actively issuing debt against its €500bn fiscal expansion, adding supply pressure that caps how much bund prices can rise even if rates fall globally. These two forces — less ECB repricing left, more German supply — mean gilts should outperform bunds. Going long gilts and short bunds isolates that differential. If bonds rally everywhere, both legs offset and the noise cancels. What remains is the UK edge.

The US–Iran MOU pulls UK rate cut expectations forward. Gilts should outperform bunds — less ECB repricing left and German supply pressure cap the European leg, while the UK energy channel drives a sharper inflation move.

Key Risks HIGH MOU collapses in the 60-day window — oil reverses, BoE holds, thesis unwinds.

HIGH UK services inflation stays sticky — BoE stays cautious regardless of the energy picture.

MED German fiscal supply pressure eases — bunds rally alongside gilts, compressing the spread.

17 Jun 2026 Hold Expected

Bank of England Rate Prediction

The Data Recent UK inflation data points to a Bank of England hold rather than an immediate rate cut. CPI stayed at 2.8%, below expectations, while CPIH remained at 3.0% and core inflation eased slightly to 2.6%. That suggests price pressures are cooling, but not disappearing entirely.

The Case for a Hold The main reason I would still expect the Bank to hold is that services inflation remains sticky at 3.7%. That matters because it shows underlying inflation is still present even as the headline rate improves. At the same time, the labour market is softening, with unemployment around 5.0% and pay growth slowing to 3.4%, which points to weaker demand and less inflationary pressure ahead.

The Outlook Overall, this feels like a mildly dovish setup. Inflation is lower than expected, the labour market is cooling, and the balance of risks is gradually shifting toward cuts later in the year. If markets begin pricing that in more aggressively, bond yields could fall and bond prices could rise.

Mixed inflation data and a weakening labour market set up a rate hold, with potential cuts later in the year — which could push bond prices higher.

11 Jun 2026 Bullish

ECB's First Rate Rise Since 2025 Lifts European Banks as Gold Slides

The ECB has raised rates for the first time since 2025, and the move keeps the policy backdrop supportive for bank earnings. Higher rates tend to lift net interest income, even if funding costs and credit risk build over time. Meanwhile gold has slipped to a six-month low, which makes sense in a stronger rate environment where demand for defensive assets fades. That combination points toward rotation into rate beneficiaries and away from safe-haven exposure.

If the ECB follows through with further hikes, European banks can keep outperforming while gold stays under pressure as capital shifts away from non-yielding assets.
